Your task is to evaluate a list of Meta targeting candidates and return only those with a strong, direct connection to the given business and its buyers.

When in doubt — reject. A smaller, high-quality list always beats a larger, diluted one.

STEP 1 — UNDERSTAND THE BUSINESS

Before evaluating any candidate, extract from the business summary:
- What product or service is being sold?
- Who is the most likely buyer? (demographics, lifestyle, behavior)
- What problem does the product solve or desire does it fulfill?
- What would a buyer be doing, interested in, or searching for before purchasing?

Use this buyer profile as your filter for every candidate.

STEP 2 — EVALUATE EACH CANDIDATE

Apply these checks in order. Fail any check → reject immediately.

A. Direct Relevance
Does this candidate have a clear, direct connection to what the business sells or who buys it?
- Direct means: someone matching this segment is meaningfully more likely to be a buyer
- Loosely related or could maybe apply is not enough → reject

B. Intent Signal
Does this candidate indicate real purchase intent, spending behavior, or life-stage relevance?

Keep if:
- Active buying behavior in the relevant category
- High discretionary spending or premium consumption
- Lifestyle strongly associated with the product
- Life stage that directly triggers this type of purchase

Reject if:
- Passive interest with no buying connection
- Generic platform behavior (device type, connectivity, app access)
- Broad demographic with no targeting precision
- Vague affinity that could apply to almost anyone

C. Geography
If this candidate is locked to a specific country or region outside the business's target market → reject.

STEP 3 — CATEGORY-SPECIFIC RULES

INTERESTS
Keep: hobbies, brand affinities, topic communities, lifestyle interests directly tied to the business domain
Reject: interests so broad they apply to a general population; any interest where the connection to the business requires more than one logical step

BEHAVIORS
Keep: engaged shoppers, frequent online buyers, high-value goods purchasers, category-specific purchase intent, lifestyle or luxury spenders, e-commerce engagement
Reject: device ownership, network or connectivity type, generic platform access patterns, any segment with no clear purchase or spending signal

DEMOGRAPHICS
Keep only when the life stage or situation directly triggers or strongly predicts a purchase:
- Life stage triggers: new parents, college students, newly married, recently moved, new homeowners, empty nesters
- Occupation: specific job roles that are direct buyers of this product
- Education: only when it correlates with income or buying behavior for this product
- Relationship or family status: only when directly tied to a life-stage purchase

Reject:
- Broad imprecise groups (e.g. "Parents – All", "Homeowners", "Adults 18–65")
- Any segment so large it offers no real targeting advantage
- Education or relationship status that does not connect to this product's buyer profile

Before keeping any demographic candidate, confirm:
1. Does being in this group make someone meaningfully more likely to buy this specific product?
2. Is this segment precise enough to offer a real targeting advantage?
3. Is the connection to the business direct — not assumed or stretched?
If any answer is NO → reject.

STEP 4 — OUTPUT

Return only the IDs of candidates that pass all checks.
Do not generate, rename, or modify any candidates.
Do not include explanations, reasoning, or commentary.
Strict JSON only.

OUTPUT FORMAT
{{
  "selected_ids": ["id1", "id2", "id3"]
}}

BUSINESS CONTEXT
{summary}

TARGETING CATEGORY
{category}

CANDIDATES
{candidates}
