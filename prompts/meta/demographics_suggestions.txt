Meta demographic segments are searched across specific sub-types in parallel:
life_events, family_statuses, income, industries, work_positions, work_employers,
education_majors, and education_statuses.

Each seed is run as a keyword search within these sub-types.
A seed only has value if it matches real segment names Meta has already indexed under one of these sub-types.

HOW META NAMES SEGMENTS IN EACH SUB-TYPE
Use these vocabulary patterns to derive seeds. Do not copy the examples — derive the right seeds from the business summary.

WORK POSITIONS
Meta indexes real job titles, not role descriptions.
Think: what is the exact job title of the buyer or decision-maker for this business?
Use title-style labels — short, clean, as they would appear on a LinkedIn profile.
Avoid seniority prefixes that are too granular (e.g. "senior", "junior", "lead") — these often return no results.

WORK EMPLOYERS
Meta indexes recognisable company names and broad employer-type labels.
Think: does this buyer work at a specific well-known company, or a recognisable employer type?
Use actual company names or employer category labels — not descriptions of the workplace.

INDUSTRIES
Meta indexes broad sector labels, not sub-sector descriptions.
Think: what is the top-level industry this buyer works in?
Use clean industry labels — not compound phrases or job function descriptions.
Keep work_positions and industries strictly separate — they search different Meta indexes.

EDUCATION MAJORS
Meta indexes academic field names as they appear on degree certificates.
Think: what field of study does this buyer typically have?
Use the degree field name — not the degree level or institution type.

EDUCATION STATUSES
Meta indexes enrollment and completion status labels.
Think: is the buyer's education status a meaningful signal for this business?
Use status labels only if they are a genuine purchase predictor.

LIFE EVENTS
Meta indexes a fixed set of named life milestones — not natural language descriptions of events.
Use milestone label-style seeds that match Meta's naming convention.
Examples of Meta's naming pattern: "new job", "newly married", "recently moved", "new baby", "away from hometown".
Derive the right milestone from the business summary — do not copy these examples verbatim.

FAMILY STATUSES
Meta indexes household and relationship status labels.
Think: is the buyer's family situation a meaningful signal for this business?
Use status labels that reflect household composition or relationship stage.

INCOME
Meta uses income bracket-style labels, not descriptive wealth terms.
Think in terms of: top percentage tiers and spending-level labels — not words like "wealthy" or "affluent".
Note: income targeting is most reliable for US audiences. For other markets, keep income seeds minimal.

CRITICAL — DO NOT COPY THE PATTERN LABELS ABOVE AS SEEDS
The sub-type descriptions above are thinking frameworks, not output templates.
Every seed must be derived from the business summary — not from the pattern descriptions or examples themselves.

IF DEMOGRAPHIC TARGETING DOES NOT CLEARLY APPLY

WHAT TO AVOID
- Interests framed as demographics (e.g. "fitness enthusiasts", "travel lovers") — use interest targeting for these
- Behaviors framed as demographics (e.g. "online shoppers", "frequent buyers") — use behavior targeting for these
- Overly broad groups that are not searchable segments (e.g. "adults", "working people")
- Job titles too granular to exist in Meta's index (e.g. "senior React developer", "junior UX designer")
- Hyper-local descriptors (e.g. city-level job markets) — Meta indexes demographics nationally
- Natural language milestone descriptions — use label-style seeds for life events
