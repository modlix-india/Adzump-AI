CRITICAL — HOW META INTEREST TARGETING WORKS
Meta indexes interests based on what people follow, engage with, and identify with on Facebook and Instagram.
Meta does NOT index product descriptions, unknown or local business names, local place names, or feature-level terminology.

Your job is to think about the buyer, not the product.

HOW META NAMES AND ORGANISES INTERESTS
Meta's interest index is built around five types of signals. Use these patterns to derive seeds:

1. BRANDS AND PLATFORMS THE BUYER USES
   Think: what are the 2-3 dominant apps, marketplaces, tools, or platforms buyers in this space already use?
   Use the actual brand or platform name — Meta indexes these directly (e.g. "Salesforce", "Nike", "Zillow").

2. MEDIA AND PUBLICATIONS THE BUYER READS
   Think: what is the industry-equivalent of a trade magazine, news outlet, or content platform this buyer follows?

3. LIFESTYLE AND HOBBY CATEGORIES THE BUYER IDENTIFIES WITH
   Think: what broader lifestyle, hobby, or identity category does this buyer belong to?
   Meta indexes these as broad category labels — use recognisable category names, not descriptions.

4. ADJACENT COMMUNITIES WITH HIGH BUYER OVERLAP
   Think: what other interest communities attract the same type of buyer, even if not directly about this product?
   These are interests that strongly correlate with the target buyer profile.

5. BROAD CATEGORY TERMS META IS KNOWN TO INDEX
   Think: what is the top-level category label for this space?
   These are short, clean category names — not product features or business descriptions.

CRITICAL — DO NOT COPY THE PATTERN LABELS ABOVE AS SEEDS
The five types above are thinking frameworks, not output templates.
Every seed must be derived from the business summary — not from the pattern descriptions themselves.

WHAT TO AVOID
- Hyper-local terms (e.g. city names, neighbourhood names) — Meta does not index these as interests
- Product feature descriptions — these are not interest segments
- Generic terms too broad to be actionable (e.g. "shopping", "business", "online")
- Demographic descriptors (e.g. "high income families", "affluent buyers")
- Behaviors or purchase signals — these belong in behavior targeting, not interests
- Job titles or professional roles — these belong in demographic targeting
- Invented terms — only generate seeds that plausibly exist in Meta's index
